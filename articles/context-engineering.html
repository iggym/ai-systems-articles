Context Engineering
Reducing Uncertainty, Not Adding Information

The underrated discipline that separates amateur prompting from production-grade AI systems
PRACTICAL + FRAMEWORKS JUNE 2026  ·  8 MIN READ  ·  7 INTERACTIVE TOOLS

One of the biggest misconceptions about context engineering is that it's about feeding AI more information. It isn't. It's about reducing uncertainty. Every piece of context should answer one of the AI's hidden questions — and knowing which questions those are is the entire discipline.

What am I trying to accomplish? What already exists? What matters most? What should I ignore? What does success look like? These are the five questions an AI model is silently trying to answer every time you send it a task. Context engineering is the art of answering them before they're asked.

The shift from prompt engineering to context engineering is like the shift from giving someone directions turn-by-turn versus handing them a map, a destination, and a list of roads that are closed. The directions solve one trip. The map solves every trip.

Analogy · The Core Idea

Imagine briefing a new surgeon who wakes up every morning with amnesia (a recurring character in this series). You could read them a long list of instructions before every procedure. Or you could build a surgical protocol — a structured system that answers every situation-dependent question before it arises. Prompt engineering reads instructions. Context engineering builds the protocol. One scales to one procedure. The other scales to a career.

"A great prompt solves one problem. A well-designed context system solves thousands of related problems — because it gives AI the same situational awareness an experienced teammate would have."

Context Debt — When Old Information Becomes a Liability

Old, conflicting, or irrelevant information quietly degrades AI quality. This is Context Debt — the hidden tax paid when stale assumptions accumulate in your context window. The AI doesn't know the information is outdated. It treats it as truth. The solution is a "Context Reset" at the start of every major task — removing stale data and summarising only what's still true.

Analogy · The Five Layers

A new employee on their first day has five things they desperately need to know: why the company exists (Goal), what the product does (Knowledge), how things are done here (Preference), what the rules and budgets are (Environmental), and what's already been tried and failed (Historical). Skip any one of those and they'll make expensive, avoidable mistakes. The five context layers are the onboarding package you build for your AI teammate — once, then reuse forever.

The Information Diet — More Context Often Means Worse Results

Counter-intuitively, adding more context often produces worse AI output. Signal drowns in noise. The model distributes attention across everything you provide — useful and irrelevant equally. Give only the information needed to make the next decision. Expand only when the current context proves insufficient.

Analogy · Information Diet

Context is fuel, not cargo. A racing car carries exactly the fuel needed to finish the race — not a full tanker. Extra fuel doesn't make you faster. It makes you heavier, slower, and more likely to run poorly. The same amount of fuel that wins a race would sink a speedboat. The optimal context load is precisely what the next decision requires — nothing more.

Negative Context — Teaching AI What Not to Do

AI needs to know what not to do as much as what to do. Most prompts are entirely positive instructions. Adding a "Never…" section — forbidden styles, assumptions, technologies, or outcomes — is one of the highest-leverage, lowest-effort improvements you can make to any prompt. Guardrails beat instructions.

Analogy · Negative Context

A new hire at a law firm needs to know what they can do — but they need equally to know what they must never do: never contact opposing counsel directly, never make representations without partner sign-off, never discuss client matters outside the office. The "Never" list isn't a restriction on capability — it's the guardrail that makes autonomy safe. The more clearly you define the edges, the more freely the AI can operate within them.

The Defining Skill of the Decade

Prompt engineering teaches AI what to do. Context engineering teaches AI how to think about your world. That is why context engineering scales. A great prompt solves one problem. A well-designed context system solves thousands of related problems by giving AI the same situational awareness an experienced teammate would have.

This shift — from crafting clever prompts to designing reusable, evolving context — is likely to be one of the defining skills of knowledge work over the rest of the decade. The engineers and knowledge workers who build this muscle first will have a compounding advantage that's genuinely difficult to replicate.

Final Analogy · The Big Picture

A great orchestra doesn't improvise from scratch at every concert. They have sheet music, rehearsal history, an agreed interpretation, a conductor's style, and decades of institutional memory about how this ensemble plays together. Each performance benefits from everything that came before. A well-engineered context system is the sheet music, rehearsal notes, and institutional memory rolled into one — the accumulated intelligence that makes every future AI interaction better than the last.
